## 1. TRIP OVERVIEW & ROUTE LOGIC

### Why These Cities?

The itinerary moves through Japan in a deliberate arc designed to **chase cherry blossoms** and experience the full spectrum of the country:

| City | Why It's on the Route | The Vibe |
|------|----------------------|----------|
| **Tokyo** | Fly in/out here. World's largest metro area. Modern + traditional sides. | Electric, endless, overwhelming (in the best way) |
| **Hakone** | Day trip from Tokyo. Mt. Fuji views, volcanic valleys, hot springs. | Mountain escape, natural beauty |
| **Takayama** | Japanese Alps mountain town. Preserved Edo-era streets. Peak cherry blossom during your dates. | Quiet, historic, sake and wagyu beef |
| **Shirakawa-go** | UNESCO village of 250-year-old thatched-roof farmhouses in a mountain valley. | Storybook rural Japan |
| **Kanazawa** | Underrated coastal city. Top-3 garden, geisha district, incredible seafood. Natural stop between Alps and Kyoto. | Elegant, artistic, refined |
| **Kyoto** | Cultural heart of Japan. Former imperial capital for 1,000 years. Temples, shrines, geisha, bamboo forests. | Timeless, romantic, spiritual |
| **Hiroshima + Miyajima** | Day trip from Kyoto. Peace Memorial + floating torii gate on an island with wild deer. | Moving, beautiful, unforgettable |
| **Osaka** | Optional buffer day. Japan's street food capital. Loud, fun, neon-lit. | Rowdy, delicious, the anti-Kyoto |

### Cherry Blossom Timing for Your Dates

| Location | First Bloom | Full Bloom | Your Dates | Status |
|----------|------------|------------|------------|--------|
| Tokyo | ~Mar 18 | ~Mar 26 | Apr 5–8 | Past peak — falling petals ("sakura fubuki"), still beautiful |
| Takayama (Alps) | ~Apr 8 | ~Apr 14 | Apr 9–10 | **PEAK BLOOM — perfect timing** |
| Kanazawa | ~Apr 3 | ~Apr 8 | Apr 11 | Late bloom — still good |
| Kyoto | ~Mar 22 | ~Mar 31 | Apr 12–16 | Late bloom / scattering — still nice |

The itinerary is designed so you hit **peak cherry blossoms in the Japanese Alps** where they bloom later due to elevation.

### DAY 4 — April 7 (Tuesday): FULL TOKYO DAY — East Meets West

**⚠️ This is your ONE full Tokyo day before Hakone. Pick your favorites from East and West side — you'll catch the rest when you return to Tokyo on Day 14.**

**Morning — East Side (Traditional Tokyo):**
- **Senso-ji Temple** & Nakamise-dori shopping street (Asakusa) — right near your hotel
  - Tokyo's oldest temple (founded 628 AD)
  - Nakamise-dori: 250m of traditional shops selling snacks, souvenirs, crafts
- Walk to **Tokyo Skytree** for panoramic city views (or admire from below — free!)
  - Ticket: ~¥2,100 for the main deck

**Lunch:** Street food at **Ameyoko Market** near Ueno — bustling market under the train tracks, cheap food stalls

**Afternoon — West Side (Modern Tokyo):**
- **Meiji Shrine** → walk through Yoyogi Park's forest paths
  - Massive Shinto shrine hidden in a 170-acre forest in the middle of Tokyo
- **Harajuku** — Takeshita Street (wild street fashion, crêpe shops, candy-colored everything)
- **Shibuya Crossing** — the world's busiest pedestrian crossing
  - Watch from above at **Shibuya Sky** observation deck (¥2,000) or the Starbucks overlooking the crossing

**🌙 Evening/Night: SHINJUKU** (Tokyo's best nightlife area)
- **Golden Gai** — an alley of ~200 tiny themed bars, each seating only 4–8 people
  - Horror bars, jazz bars, manga bars, 80s music bars — each has a personality
  - Cover charge: ¥300–1,000. Drinks: ¥500–800. Just bar-hop and explore
  - Go around 8–9 PM for the best atmosphere
- **Omoide Yokocho** ("Memory Lane" / "Piss Alley") — smoky yakitori (grilled chicken skewer) stalls
  - Grab a stool elbow-to-elbow with salarymen
  - Beer + a plate of yakitori skewers: ~¥1,000
- Return to hotel for **free late-night ramen** at Dormy Inn

**Saved for return (Day 14 evening or Day 15 morning):** Akihabara, Ueno Park, Yanaka Ginza, Shimokitazawa, or anything you didn't get to today.

🌿 **Relaxed alt:** Senso-ji morning + Meiji Shrine afternoon is a perfect half-and-half day. Skip Shibuya and have a quiet izakaya dinner near Asakusa instead.

### DAY 5 — April 8 (Wednesday): DAY TRIP — HAKONE (Mt. Fuji Views & Hot Springs)

**🎫 ACTIVATE YOUR 14-DAY JR PASS** at Tokyo Station JR counter first thing (~8 AM). Bring your exchange voucher and passports.

**Daytime:**
- JR Shinkansen Tokyo → Odawara (~35 min, **JR Pass** ✓)
- Buy **Hakone Free Pass** at Odawara Station (**¥6,000/person** 2-day pass, as of Mar 2026; separate from JR Pass)
- **Hakone Loop** (follow this circuit):
  1. **Switchback train** Odawara → Gora (scenic mountain railway)
  2. **Cable car** Gora → Owakudani volcanic valley
     - Try the **black sulfur eggs** — cooked in volcanic steam, supposedly add 7 years to your life!
     - Volcanic steam vents, sulfur smell, dramatic moonscape
  3. **Ropeway** over the mountains → Togendai (aerial gondola with mountain panoramas)
  4. **Lake Ashi pirate ship cruise** — Mt. Fuji views on clear days across the lake
  5. Bus back to Odawara
- Optional: **Hakone Open-Air Museum** — sculptures + Picasso collection in a stunning mountain setting
- Late afternoon: Stop at a **day-use onsen** before returning
  - **Tenzan Tohji-kyo** near Hakone-Yumoto (¥1,300 entry) — excellent outdoor baths in a forest setting

**🌙 Evening/Night:**
- Return to Tokyo by JR Shinkansen Odawara → Tokyo (**JR Pass** ✓)
- **IMPORTANT: Send luggage ahead tonight!**
  - Arrange **takkyubin** (luggage forwarding) at Dormy Inn front desk
  - Send big bags to your **Kyoto hotel** (~¥2,000/bag, arrives in 1–2 days)
  - Pack daypacks only for the Alps leg (Takayama/Kanazawa = 3 nights)
  - Ask the front desk for "takkyubin" — they'll handle everything
- **Last free late-night ramen** at Dormy Inn 😢
- Pack and prep for Takayama departure tomorrow

> **🔄 SUBSTITUTE: KAMAKURA** (instead of Hakone)
> Ancient capital (1185–1333) on the coast, 1 hour from Tokyo by JR (**JR Pass** ✓)
> - **Great Buddha** (Kotoku-in) — iconic 13m bronze statue sitting outdoors
> - Tsurugaoka Hachimangu shrine — grand approach lined with cherry trees
> - Hokoku-ji — "Bamboo Temple" with a bamboo grove + matcha tea garden
> - Seaside town vibe, can walk along Yuigahama Beach
> - **Easier logistics** than Hakone (no separate pass needed, everything JR or walking)
> - Less dramatic scenery but more cultural depth + ocean views
>
> **🔄 SUBSTITUTE: NIKKO** (instead of Hakone)
> Elaborate Toshogu Shrine complex in cedar forests (UNESCO), 2 hours from Tokyo by JR (**JR Pass** ✓)
> - Ornate, gold-covered shrine buildings with famous "hear no evil, see no evil, speak no evil" monkey carvings
> - Kegon Falls and Lake Chuzenji
> - More nature-focused, good for hiking
> - Farther away = longer day, but worth it if you love forests and shrines

### DAY 6 — April 9 (Thursday): TOKYO → TAKAYAMA (Japanese Alps)

**Daytime:**
- Check out of Dormy Inn Asakusa (bags already sent to Kyoto via takkyubin — travel with daypacks!)
- **JR Shinkansen Tokyo → Nagoya** (~1h 40min, **JR Pass** ✓, use Hikari — not Nozomi)
- **JR Hida Limited Express Nagoya → Takayama** (~2h 20min, **JR Pass** ✓)
  - **This is one of Japan's most scenic train rides** — the train winds through mountain gorges, crosses rivers, passes through tiny villages. Sit by the window and enjoy.
- Arrive Takayama early afternoon
- Check into your **ryokan** (traditional Japanese inn)
- Afternoon: Explore **Sanmachi Suji** historic district
  - Preserved Edo-era merchant streets — dark wooden buildings, narrow alleys, willow trees
  - **Sake breweries** — look for *sugidama* (cedar balls) hanging outside indicating new sake
  - Many offer tastings: ¥200–500 for a dozen varieties. You WILL buy bottles.
  - Craft shops, small galleries, pickled vegetable shops
- Visit **Takayama Jinya** — beautifully preserved Edo-period government building with a rice storehouse

**🌸 CHERRY BLOSSOMS: Takayama should be at or near PEAK BLOOM during your visit!**

**🌙 Evening/Night: RYOKAN EXPERIENCE** (the highlight of the trip)
- **Multi-course kaiseki dinner** served in your room or a private dining area
  - 8–12 courses of seasonal Hida cuisine
  - **Hida beef** (the region's famous wagyu) will be featured — possibly as sashimi, grilled, or in hoba miso
  - Seasonal vegetables, mountain stream fish, tofu, rice, miso soup, pickles
  - This is culinary art. Take your time. Take photos.
- After dinner: soak in the **onsen bath** — indoor and/or outdoor depending on the ryokan
  - Most ryokans have gender-separated baths
  - Ask about **kashikiri** (private bath reservation) if you want to soak together
- Sleep on **futon** laid out on **tatami mats** by the staff while you were at dinner
- **This will be one of the most memorable nights of the entire trip.**

### DAY 7 — April 10 (Friday): FULL DAY TAKAYAMA

**Daytime:**
- Early morning: **Miyagawa Morning Market** (opens 6 AM, runs until noon)
  - Local farmers selling produce, pickles, crafts, and miso along the riverside
  - Try apple butter, mountain vegetable pickles, handmade crafts
- Breakfast: Included at ryokan (traditional Japanese breakfast — rice, miso soup, grilled fish, pickles, egg). Check out after breakfast.
- Check into **K's House Takayama**
- Late morning: **Hida Folk Village** (Hida no Sato) — open-air museum with 30+ traditional buildings on a hillside overlooking the Japanese Alps
  - Same gassho-zukuri (thatched roof) architecture as Shirakawa-go
  - Beautiful hillside setting with mountain panoramas
- Lunch: **Hida beef sushi** (slices of seared wagyu on rice, eaten by hand — ~¥600–900 for 2 pieces) or **hoba miso** (beef and vegetables grilled on a magnolia leaf over charcoal)
- Afternoon: More Sanmachi exploration, sake brewery visits, **Takayama Festival Floats Museum** (Yatai Kaikan) — elaborate festival floats with intricate mechanical puppets

**🌙 Evening/Night:**
- Walk the old streets at night — **lantern-lit, nearly empty, completely different atmosphere** than daytime
- Dinner at a local izakaya — try Hida beef in another preparation (sukiyaki, shabu-shabu, or as sushi again — it's that good)
- The town is quiet at night. Embrace it. This is the opposite of Tokyo, and that's the point.

🌿 **Relaxed alt:** Skip Hida Folk Village and spend a full lazy day. Morning market, old town, sake breweries, long lunch, afternoon at one of Takayama's small day-use onsen.

### DAY 8 — April 11 (Saturday): TAKAYAMA → SHIRAKAWA-GO → KANAZAWA

**⚠️ NOHI BUS MUST BE PRE-BOOKED — sells out in cherry blossom season!**
Reserve at **nouhibus.co.jp** or **japanbusonline.com** (opens 1 month prior). Phone: +81-577-32-1688 (9 AM–6 PM JST).
You need TWO bus segments (fares as of Mar 2026 — verify at booking):
1. Takayama → Shirakawa-go (~50 min, **¥2,800**/person, Nohi Bus)
2. Shirakawa-go → Kanazawa (~1h 15min, **¥2,800**/person, Nohi Bus)

**Daytime:**
- Check out of K's House Takayama
- Morning: **Nohi Bus Takayama → Shirakawa-go** (~50 min)
- **Shirakawa-go UNESCO Village** (explore 2–3 hours):
  - Walk among **gassho-zukuri farmhouses** — steep thatched roofs built 250+ years ago to handle heavy snowfall
  - Visit **Wada House** (largest preserved farmhouse, open to public, ~¥300)
  - **Hike to the observation deck** for the iconic panoramic view — this is THE photo of the trip
  - Lunch in the village — try soba noodles or gohei-mochi (grilled rice cakes with walnut-miso glaze)
- Afternoon: **Nohi Bus Shirakawa-go → Kanazawa** (~1h 15min, ¥2,800/person)
- Arrive Kanazawa mid-afternoon
- Check into **Kaname Inn Tatemachi**
- Afternoon: **Kenrokuen Garden** — one of Japan's top 3 gardens
  - Possible late cherry blossoms! The garden's cherry trees bloom slightly later than the city.
  - Take your time — this garden rewards slow wandering
  - Walk through **Kanazawa Castle Park** grounds next door

**🌙 Evening/Night:**
- **Higashi Chaya (Geisha) District** — atmospheric wooden teahouses lit by warm lanterns at dusk
  - You may hear shamisen (three-stringed instrument) music drifting from inside
  - The architecture is beautifully preserved — this feels like stepping into old Japan
  - Some teahouses are open to visitors during the day; at night, just walk and absorb the atmosphere
- Dinner: **Fresh seafood at Omicho Market** — "the kitchen of Kanazawa"
  - Kanazawa is on the Sea of Japan coast = exceptional sushi and sashimi
  - Try the **kaisendon** (sashimi rice bowl) — seasonal fish piled high on rice
  - Snow crab if in season, buri (yellowtail), nodoguro (blackthroat seaperch)
- Walk along the Sai River after dinner — quiet and atmospheric

> **🔄 SUBSTITUTE FOR SHIRAKAWA-GO: HIDA FOLK VILLAGE**
> If Nohi Bus is sold out or weather is bad:
> - Located IN Takayama (~10 min by bus/taxi from town center)
> - Same gassho-zukuri farmhouse architecture as Shirakawa-go
> - Hillside setting with mountain views, fewer crowds
> - Then take JR trains Takayama → Nagoya → Kanazawa (longer but all JR Pass covered)
>
> **🔄 SUBSTITUTE: SKIP KANAZAWA ENTIRELY**
> - Take JR Hida Express Takayama → Nagoya, then Shinkansen Nagoya → Kyoto (~4.5 hrs total, all **JR Pass** ✓)
> - Saves 1 hotel change + ~$60/night + Nohi Bus cost
> - Gives you an EXTRA day in Kyoto
> - **You miss:** Kenrokuen Garden, geisha district, Omicho Market seafood, gold leaf ice cream
> - **Good option if:** you're tired of moving, want more Kyoto time, or Nohi Bus is sold out

### DAY 9 — April 12 (Sunday): KANAZAWA → KYOTO

**Daytime:**
- Morning: **21st Century Museum of Contemporary Art** — free outdoor installations, ticketed indoor exhibits
  - The "Swimming Pool" installation (you look down through glass at people who appear to be underwater) is iconic
- **Off the beaten path:** D.T. Suzuki Museum — serene, minimalist museum of Zen Buddhism with a stunning reflective water garden. Perfect for quiet contemplation. One of the most peaceful spaces in Japan.
- Late morning: **Nagamachi Samurai District** — historic samurai residences with earthen walls and narrow stone-lined canals
- **Try gold leaf ice cream** at Hakuichi — an entire sheet of gold leaf on soft-serve. Kanazawa produces 99% of Japan's gold leaf. ~¥900.
- Lunch: Last Omicho Market meal, or try a Kanazawa curry (the city has its own distinct curry style)
- Afternoon: **Train to Kyoto**
  - Route (changed March 2024): **Hokuriku Shinkansen** Kanazawa → Tsuruga (~35–45 min), then **Thunderbird** limited express Tsuruga → Kyoto (~75–80 min). Both covered by **JR Pass** ✓.
  - Use **Hakutaka** (faster, ~35 min) or **Tsurugi** (local shinkansen, ~45 min). **Kagayaki** is also JR Pass-covered — some Kagayaki services continue beyond Kanazawa to Tsuruga (stopping at Fukui), while others terminate at Kanazawa. Check the timetable at jreast.co.jp or use Google Maps to confirm your specific train serves the Kanazawa→Tsuruga segment.
  - Transfer at Tsuruga is designed to be seamless — same platform or adjacent platforms, timed connections, 5–10 min to transfer.
  - Total: ~2h 00–2h 20min with transfer
- Arrive Kyoto early evening
- Check into **Piece Hostel Sanjo**

**🌙 Evening/Night:**
- **Stroll along the Kamo River** — locals sit along the riverbanks at dusk, very peaceful. One of Kyoto's signature scenes.
- Walk to **Pontocho Alley** — a narrow, lantern-lit alley of restaurants running parallel to the Kamo River
  - Many restaurants have **kamogawa terraces** — outdoor riverside deck seating
  - Find a spot with river views for your first Kyoto dinner
  - Price range: ¥2,000–5,000/person depending on the restaurant
- Orientation walk through the Sanjo/Shijo area to get your bearings

🌿 **Relaxed alt:** If you skipped Kanazawa and arrived in Kyoto earlier, use the morning as a free day — sleep in, wander Nishiki Market, sit in a café.

### DAY 10 — April 13 (Monday): KYOTO DAY 1 — Eastern Temples & Gion

**Daytime:**
- **GO EARLY: Fushimi Inari Shrine at ~6:30 AM**
  - Thousands of orange torii gates winding up a mountainside
  - The difference between 6:30 AM (nearly empty, magical, your photos look incredible) and 9:00 AM (shoulder-to-shoulder tourist crowds) is ENORMOUS
  - Full hike to the summit and back: ~2–3 hours. The higher you go, the fewer people.
  - **Keihan Line** from Sanjo Station to Fushimi-Inari Station (~6 min, direct, ~¥220), or walk (~25 min from Piece Hostel)
- Mid-morning: **Kiyomizu-dera Temple** — iconic wooden stage jutting out from a hillside with panoramic city views
  - Entry: ¥400
  - The engineering is staggering — the stage is supported by 139 pillars with no nails
- Walk down **Sannenzaka & Ninenzaka** — atmospheric stone-paved lanes with traditional shops, tea houses, pottery
  - This is peak "old Kyoto" — the streets are lined with wooden machiya buildings
  - Great for picking up ceramics, fans, incense, matcha sweets
- Lunch: In the Higashiyama area — plenty of small restaurants and tea houses
- Afternoon: **Philosopher's Path** — canal-side walking path (possible late cherry blossoms)
  - 2 km path lined with cherry trees and small temples
  - Leads to **Ginkaku-ji** (Silver Pavilion) — serene moss garden, raked sand garden

**🌙 Evening/Night: GION** — Kyoto's geisha quarter
- Walk **Hanamikoji Street** at dusk
  - Chance to spot **geiko** (full geisha) or **maiko** (apprentice geisha) heading to evening engagements in full dress — white face makeup, elaborate kimono, distinctive hairstyle
  - **Be respectful** — do NOT chase, block their path, or photograph them up close. They are working professionals, not performers.
  - The sighting is magical even from a respectful distance
  - Best time: 5:30–7:00 PM as they walk to engagements
- Atmospheric wooden machiya buildings lit by paper lanterns
- Dinner: Gion area restaurant, or return to **Pontocho Alley** for riverside dining
- **Walk along the Kamo River at night** — couples sit along the banks, the water reflects city lights. One of the most romantic walks in Japan.

🌿 **Relaxed alt:** Fushimi Inari in the morning (1.5–2 hrs), then Higashiyama area for Kiyomizu-dera + the lanes. Have a long lunch. That's a full, satisfying day without rushing.

### DAY 11 — April 14 (Tuesday): KYOTO DAY 2 — Arashiyama & Western Kyoto

**Daytime:**
- Morning: **Kinkaku-ji (Golden Pavilion)** — a gold-leafed temple reflected perfectly in a still pond. One of Japan's most iconic sights.
  - Entry: ¥500. Get there ~9 AM for best light and fewer people.
- Train or bus to **Arashiyama** (western Kyoto):
  - **Bamboo Grove** — towering bamboo stalks creating a green cathedral
    - Go early (~8 AM) or late afternoon (~4 PM) to avoid peak crowds
    - The sound of wind through bamboo is otherworldly
  - **Tenryu-ji Temple** (UNESCO) — stunning zen rock garden with mountain backdrop
  - **Togetsukyo Bridge** — scenic bridge with mountain views, iconic Arashiyama photo spot
- Lunch: Riverside restaurant in Arashiyama — yudofu (simmered tofu, Kyoto specialty) or tempura
- Afternoon: **Monkey Park Iwatayama** — short hike up a mountain for panoramic views + wild Japanese macaques
  - You can feed the monkeys from inside an enclosed hut (they're outside, you're inside — reversed zoo)
  - Great views of Kyoto from the top

**🌙 Evening/Night:**
- **Nishiki Market** ("Kyoto's Kitchen") — 5 blocks of food stalls and shops
  - Try: dashimaki tamago (fluffy rolled omelette), fresh mochi, matcha soft serve, Kyoto pickles, grilled seafood on sticks
  - Good for souvenir shopping — packaged tea, spices, ceramics
  - Open until ~5–6 PM, so catch it in late afternoon
- **Check into Airbnb machiya** (if switching from Piece Hostel today)
  - Settle into your traditional Kyoto townhouse — sliding shoji screens, tatami floors, perhaps a small garden
  - This is the **most romantic accommodation** on the trip
- Quiet evening in your machiya — pick up bento or takeout and eat "at home"

> **Off the beaten path:** Kurama to Kibune hike — 20 min train from Arashiyama to Kurama. Hike the mountain trail through ancient cedar forest (~1.5 hrs) to Kibune village. Tiny shrines along the way. In Kibune, restaurants serve food on platforms over a rushing stream (kawadoko dining, seasonal). A hidden gem.

🌿 **Relaxed alt:** Arashiyama alone fills a wonderful full day. Bamboo grove, temple, bridge, monkey park, riverside lunch. Skip Kinkaku-ji (do it another day or skip it — you can find the image on any postcard).

### DAY 12 — April 15 (Wednesday): DAY TRIP — HIROSHIMA & MIYAJIMA ISLAND

**⚠️ This is a long day. See substitutes below if the pace feels like too much.**

**Daytime:**
- Early: **JR Shinkansen Kyoto → Hiroshima** (~1h 45min on Hikari, **JR Pass** ✓)
  - Use Hikari (not Nozomi — JR Pass doesn't cover Nozomi)
  - Depart ~7:30–8:00 AM to maximize your day
- Morning: **Hiroshima Peace Memorial Park and Museum** (~2 hours)
  - Deeply moving. The museum tells individual human stories — letters, belongings, shadows burned into stone.
  - Take your time. It's emotional. It's important.
  - See the **A-Bomb Dome** (UNESCO) — the only structure left standing near the hypocenter, preserved as a memorial
  - Free to walk the park; museum entry ¥200
- Lunch: **Hiroshima-style okonomiyaki** — savory pancake layered with noodles, cabbage, pork, egg, sauce
  - Different from Osaka style (Osaka mixes; Hiroshima layers)
  - Okonomimura building near Peace Park has dozens of stalls — pick any busy one
- Afternoon: **JR ferry to Miyajima Island** (~10 min from Miyajimaguchi Station, **JR Pass covers the JR ferry** ✓)
  - **"Floating" Itsukushima Torii Gate** — the iconic red gate standing in the water (recently restored in 2022)
  - At high tide it appears to float; at low tide you can walk to it
  - Friendly **wild deer** roam the island — they'll walk right up to you
  - Try **momiji manju** — maple leaf-shaped cakes with various fillings (red bean, custard, chocolate)
  - Walk the charming shopping street to Itsukushima Shrine
- Return: JR ferry + Shinkansen Hiroshima → Kyoto (**JR Pass** ✓), arrive by ~7–8 PM

**🌙 Night:** Quiet evening. You'll be tired. Enjoy your machiya.

> **🔄 SUBSTITUTE: NARA (much easier day)**
> JR train Kyoto → Nara (~45 min, **JR Pass** ✓)
> - **Todai-ji Temple** — world's largest wooden building housing a 15m bronze Buddha. Jaw-dropping scale.
> - **Nara Park** — 1,200+ sacred deer that **BOW when you offer them crackers** (¥200/pack from vendors). Not a joke — they actually bow.
> - **Kasuga-taisha Shrine** — path lined with hundreds of moss-covered stone lanterns
> - Compact, walkable, very peaceful. **Much lower energy** than the Hiroshima trip.
> - Great for a day when you want culture without exhaustion.
>
> **🔄 SUBSTITUTE: HIMEJI CASTLE + NARA COMBO**
> - Morning: JR Shinkansen to Himeji (~50 min, **JR Pass** ✓) — Japan's most spectacular original castle. White walls, soaring architecture, recently restored.
> - Afternoon: JR train Himeji → Nara (~1 hr) — Todai-ji + deer park
> - Evening: JR train Nara → Kyoto (~45 min)
> - Ambitious day but hits two bucket-list sights.

## 8. CULTURAL TIPS & ETIQUETTE

### The Big Rules

| Rule | Why It Matters |
|------|---------------|
| **Shoes off indoors** | Remove shoes when entering homes, ryokan, many restaurants, temples. Look for a step up and a shoe rack. Use slippers provided. Separate toilet slippers in bathrooms — switch back! |
| **No tipping. Ever.** | Tipping is not customary and can cause confusion or embarrassment. Service is already excellent. The price is the price. |
| **Quiet on trains** | Phone calls on trains are a major faux pas. Set phones to silent ("manner mode"). Speak in low voices. Eating on local trains is frowned upon (Shinkansen = OK, bento eating is encouraged). |
| **Bowing** | A slight 15-degree nod for greetings, thanks, apologies. When someone bows to you, bow back. Don't overthink it. |
| **Don't eat while walking** | Eat at the stall where you bought street food, or at a designated area. Standing and eating = fine. Walking and eating = not fine. |

### Onsen (Hot Spring) Etiquette

You'll use onsen at Dormy Inn, the ryokan, and possibly day-use facilities. Here's how:

1. **Wash thoroughly** at the shower stations BEFORE entering the bath. The bath is for soaking, not washing.
2. **No swimsuits.** Onsen are nude. Everyone is. It feels awkward for 30 seconds, then it's completely normal.
3. **Small towel:** Bring it to the bath area but do NOT put it in the water. Fold it on your head or set it on the bath's edge.
4. **Tattoo note:** Some onsen prohibit visible tattoos (historical yakuza association). Many have relaxed this for tourists. Ask at the front desk. **Private onsen rooms (kashikiri)** are an alternative — you and your wife can use one together. The ryokan likely offers this.
5. **Gender-separated:** Most onsen have separate men's and women's baths. Kashikiri (private) baths allow couples.

### Temple & Shrine Etiquette

**Shrines (Shinto) — marked by torii gates:**
1. Bow slightly at the entrance gate
2. Purification fountain (temizu): rinse left hand → right hand → pour water in left hand, rinse mouth → rinse left hand again
3. At the offering hall: toss a coin (¥5 is lucky), bow twice, clap twice, make your wish, bow once

**Temples (Buddhist) — marked by gate guardians:**
1. Bow at the gate
2. Light incense if available, waft smoke toward you (believed healing)
3. Put hands together and bow at the altar. **No clapping** at temples (that's for shrines)

### 5 Japanese Phrases That Go a LONG Way

| Phrase | Pronunciation | Meaning | When to Use |
|--------|--------------|---------|-------------|
| Konnichiwa | kohn-nee-chee-wah | Hello | Greeting anyone |
| Arigato gozaimasu | ah-ree-gah-toh go-zah-ee-mahs | Thank you very much | Constantly |
| Sumimasen | sue-mee-mah-sen | Excuse me / I'm sorry | Getting attention, apologizing, everything |
| Eigo wa daijoubu desu ka? | ay-go wah dye-joh-boo des kah | Is English OK? | Before asking a question |
| Oishii! | oy-shee | Delicious! | Say to the chef — they love it |

### Money & Payment

- **Japan is more cash-based than you'd expect.** Many small restaurants, market stalls, shrines, and rural businesses are cash-only.
- **Always carry ¥10,000–30,000 in cash** (~$65–200)
- **ATMs:** 7-Eleven ATMs are the most reliable for international cards. Available 24/7.
- **IC cards** (Suica/Pasmo) work at convenience stores, vending machines, coin lockers, and many chain restaurants.
- **Credit cards:** Accepted at department stores, hotels, and larger restaurants. Visa and Mastercard most widely accepted. Amex less common outside hotels.
- **Change trays:** Place money on the small tray at the register, don't hand it directly.
- **Notify your bank** of Japan travel dates before departure to avoid card blocks.

### Weather & Clothing (April)

| Location | Highs | Lows | What to Wear |
|----------|-------|------|-------------|
| Tokyo/Kyoto/Osaka | 60–70°F (15–21°C) | 45–55°F (7–13°C) | Layers: t-shirt + light jacket |

- **Rain is likely.** April averages 8–10 rainy days. Pack a compact umbrella or buy a clear vinyl one from any convenience store for ¥500 (the classic Japanese umbrella).

- **Slip-on shoes** are helpful for temples and traditional spaces where you remove shoes frequently.
- **Layers:** The temperature swings ~20°F between morning and afternoon. Carry a light jacket in your daypack.

### Toilets

Japanese toilets are legendary. Heated seats, bidet, dryer, sound effects (to mask noise). Buttons are usually labeled in English. The big button with water waves = bidet. Look before you sit — the lid may auto-open.

Public toilets are everywhere, almost always clean, and always free. Train stations, department stores, convenience stores, parks. Carry a small tissue pack just in case (some don't have hand dryers or paper towels).

### Safety

Japan is **extremely safe**. You can leave belongings at a café table while ordering. Lost wallets are often returned with cash inside. That said, use normal urban common sense.

- **Trash cans are rare.** Carry a small bag for trash. Bins exist at convenience stores and train stations but not on streets.
- **Queuing:** Japanese people queue for everything. Follow the lines.
- **Escalators:** Stand on the LEFT in Tokyo (right side for walking). In Osaka, it's REVERSED — stand on the RIGHT.
- **Coin lockers:** Available at all major train stations (¥300–700 depending on size). Great for storing bags during day trips. Use IC card to pay.

### Power & Adapters

**No power adapter needed!** Japan uses Type A plugs (same as US). Voltage is 100V vs US 120V — all modern electronics handle this fine (check the label on your charger — it'll say "100–240V").

### Restaurant Tips

- **Ticket vending machines:** Many ramen shops and gyudon chains have ticket machines at the entrance. Insert money → press button for what you want (usually with pictures) → hand ticket to chef. No need to speak.
- **Ordering:** Many restaurants have picture menus or plastic food displays in the window. Point at what you want.
- **Water is free.** Served automatically in most restaurants.
- **"Oshibori"** — hot or cold wet towel served before your meal. Wipe your hands. Do not wipe your face (considered rude in nicer restaurants, fine in casual spots).
- **Saying "gochisousama deshita"** (go-chee-soh-sah-mah desh-tah) when leaving means "thank you for the meal" — the staff will love it.
- **Reservations:** For popular restaurants, book via Tabelog or ask your hotel front desk to call. Many small restaurants are first-come-first-served.

## 12. WHAT TO DO IF...

| Situation | Solution |
|-----------|----------|
| **Nohi Bus is sold out** | **Option A:** Skip Kanazawa — take JR Hida Express Takayama → Nagoya, then Tokaido Shinkansen (Hikari) Nagoya → Kyoto (~4.5 hrs total, all **JR Pass** ✓). Gains an extra Kyoto day. **Option B:** JR Hida Express Takayama → Nagoya, Shinkansen to Maibara, JR Shirasagi to Tsuruga, Hokuriku Shinkansen Tsuruga → Kanazawa (all JR Pass, ~5–6 hrs, complex but doable). |
| **Ryokan in Takayama is sold out** | Try Japanican, Booking.com, and Rakuten Travel (travel.rakuten.co.jp — Japanese platform with inventory not on Western sites). Or try a ryokan in nearby Gero Onsen (JR Pass, 1 hr from Takayama). |
| **Piece Hostel Kyoto private rooms are full** | Book K's House Kyoto private room ($45–60) or search Booking.com for "private room Kyoto" filtered by your dates and budget. |
| **Rain on Hakone day** | Ropeway may close in bad weather. Swap Hakone for Kamakura (indoor temples + covered shopping streets) or stay in Tokyo (museums: Mori Art Museum, Teamlab, Shibuya Sky). |
| **You get sick or exhausted** | Rest. Japan has excellent pharmacies — look for green cross signs. Convenience stores sell basic medicine. Drink Pocari Sweat (electrolyte drink). Hotels can call an English-speaking doctor. |
| **You miss a train** | Don't panic. Another one comes in 10–30 minutes on major routes. JR Pass covers any JR train — just take the next one. For reserved seats, go to the non-reserved car. |
| **Lost something on a train** | Report to station staff immediately. Japan's Lost & Found system is remarkably effective. Items are held at the terminal station. |
| **Credit card gets blocked** | Use a 7-Eleven ATM to withdraw cash (they reliably accept international cards). Call your bank to unblock. This is why you carry cash. |
| **Can't read a menu** | Use Google Translate camera mode — point your phone at the menu and it translates in real-time. Or look for restaurants with plastic food displays in the window and point. |
| **Need to contact each other but got separated** | Agree on a meeting point and time in advance. "Meet at the ticket gates of [station name] in 30 minutes" works. If you have pocket WiFi, both phones work. If eSIM, you're each independent. |
| **Luggage forwarding hasn't arrived** | Extremely rare (99.9% on-time), but if it happens, call the tracking number on your receipt. Your hotel front desk can help. Wear the clothes you packed in your daypack. |

## 13. CONVERSATION CHEAT SHEET

Beyond the basics — useful phrases for specific situations:

| Situation | Japanese | Pronunciation | English |
|-----------|----------|--------------|---------|
| Entering a shop/restaurant | Irasshaimase! | ee-rah-shy-mah-seh | (They say this to YOU — just smile/nod) |
| "Check please" | Okaikei onegaishimasu | oh-kai-kay oh-neh-guy-shee-mahs | May I have the bill? |
| "Where is ___?" | ___ wa doko desu ka? | ___ wah doh-koh des kah | Where is ___? |
| "How much?" | Ikura desu ka? | ee-koo-rah des kah | How much is this? |
| "This one please" | Kore o kudasai | koh-reh oh koo-dah-sai | I'd like this one |
| "Two people" | Futari desu | foo-tah-ree des | Table for two |
| "No [allergen]" | ___ nashi de | ___ nah-shee deh | Without ___ |
| "Cheers!" | Kanpai! | kahn-pai | Cheers! (before drinking) |
| Leaving a restaurant | Gochisousama deshita | go-chee-soh-sah-mah desh-tah | Thank you for the meal |
| "Beautiful!" | Kirei! | kee-ray | Beautiful/Pretty |
| "It was fun!" | Tanoshikatta! | tah-noh-shee-kah-tah | I had fun! |
| Apologizing (bumped someone) | Sumimasen | sue-mee-mah-sen | Excuse me / Sorry |
